Context

"The first message was a LinkedIn DM from the CEO. No design existed. No system. No direction. Just an idea and a team of engineers waiting."

Rafikii started as a sister company of Africarriers Group, owned by Mr. Raza. The core problem: property managers in Tanzania were drowning in fragmented invoices, cash payments, and zero visibility into their portfolio performance. Tenants had no digital way to pay rent, top up electricity tokens, or track their lease.

I joined as the founding and only designer, collaborating with an India-based mobile dev, a backend team, and the CEO directly. My job: figure out what to build, design it, and get it shipped.

The Problem Space

🏠 Tenants
Paying rent meant bank transfers, cash deliveries, or mobile money — each handled differently per landlord. LUKU electricity tokens required going to a physical meter. No single place to see your lease, dues, or maintenance requests.
✅ What We Built
A mobile app where tenants manage everything: rent, electricity top-up, utility bills, invoice tracking, complaints, and family member access — all in one place, via MoMo or card.
🏢 Property Managers
Hundreds of invoices per month, received in broken fragments — cash, M-Pesa, bank. No way to track which tenant paid what. Proforma to final invoice conversion done manually. Zero data on business growth or occupancy trends.
✅ What We Built
A full property management dashboard: smart invoicing, automated payment reminders, lease management, maintenance tracking, multi-currency support, and a live analytics view of the whole portfolio.
📊 Business Owners
No cross-product visibility. Revenue came in from MoMo, cards, and cash with no unified view. Impossible to track operator performance, identify late payers at scale, or make data-driven decisions.
✅ What We Built
Mission Control — a centralized admin dashboard tracking all payments across both products, broken down by operator (Vodacom, Airtel, TigoPesa), with a full ticketing system for user support.

Key Design Decisions

💡 Decision: One App, Multiple User Types
The hardest call was deciding to build separate products for tenants vs managers, rather than one combined app. A combined app would have been faster to ship, but the mental models were completely different. Tenants think in "pay my rent." Managers think in "collect revenue across 300 units." Trying to serve both in one interface would have failed both. Separate products, shared design system.
💡 Decision: MoMo-First Payment Design
Tanzania runs on mobile money. We designed the payment flow around MoMo as the primary path — not card. This meant the whole flow: selecting operator (Vodacom, Airtel, TigoPesa), entering phone number, confirming — had to feel as natural as sending a WhatsApp message. Cards were a secondary tab, not the default. This matched how real Tanzanians actually pay.
💡 Decision: Mission Control as a Separate Product
Originally, admin reporting was going to be a section inside the property manager dashboard. I pushed back. The people who need mission control — investors, business owners, operations leads — have completely different goals than property managers. Mixing them would create complexity for both. A dedicated Mission Control product gave each audience clarity.

Key Design Decisions

💡 Decision: Different Visual Language Per Vertical
The biggest challenge: Flights and Movies are completely different emotional contexts. Booking a flight is transactional — people want clarity, trust, and speed. Booking a movie is about excitement and entertainment. I gave each vertical its own visual energy while keeping the underlying navigation and booking flow consistent. Flights: clean white, functional. Movies: dark cinematic, full-bleed poster art. Events: editorial, editorial bold. Same design system. Different personalities.
💡 Decision: Unified Navigation Across Web & Mobile
Otapp needed to work as a web platform AND a mobile app. Most designers would design these separately. I designed one navigation system — the horizontal pill nav — that adapts to both. Same labels, same order, same logic. Users who switch between app and web don't have to relearn anything. This was a conscious product decision, not just a visual one.
💡 Decision: Progressive Disclosure in Booking Flows
The old app showed everything at once — overwhelming. The new flows use progressive disclosure: select movie → pick date → choose showtime → select seats (or format first via modal) → confirm. Each step is one clear decision. Contextual modals handle sub-choices (format: 2D/3D, number of seats) without losing your place in the flow.
